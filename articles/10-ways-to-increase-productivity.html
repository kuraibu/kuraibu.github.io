10 Ways To Increase Your Productivity

by Clive Tshuma -August 05th, 2018

Web and software development is, at least on paper, quite a straight forward job.

Make plan, learn syntax, write code, get results…

Ok, maybe that’s a bit too much of an over simplification, but you get the gist of it.

  1. Don't Over Plan

    So, I mentioned over-planning first because it is probably the easiest trap anyone in web design and development can fall into. I know I myself (and probably many of you) have fallen victim to over-planning on a number of occasions. There is the common misconception that the more we plan the better the implementation will be, so plan plan plan. Sometimes, planning becomes a sort of comfort zone and getting out of that comfort zone becomes a serious challenge.

    Often over-planning is caused by fear. Fear that your idea may not be received well. In reality, we all know how absurd this is. The one thing worse than our ideas not being received well, is our ideas not being received at all. So measures need to be taken to help us break out of our comfort zone.

    Before you start planning any much else. Set a date for implementation. I know this might sound unrealistic to many. How can we set deadlines if we don’t even know how we are going to approach the project.

    The fact that you have set a deadline doesn’t mean you have to finish everything on that date. Make practical (non-planning) steps in time for your deadline. If you need more time for further steps you can always make more implementation deadlines for the extra steps. Setting time for yourself were you have to take actual, non-planning steps towards your goal makes your dream a reality and pulls you from the realm of perpetual planning.

  2. Don't Under Plan

    The first point may have caused some misunderstandings so I am going to go right ahead and try to clear those up right now.

    I’ll get right to the point. Going into a project without a clear adequate plan is toxic for your project.

    Yes, I did encourage people to set implementation deadlines early. That is only to ensure that you are going to take some practical active steps by a particular time. And remember, that was meant for those that have trouble getting started because they cannot move forward from the planning stage.

    Now for those of you that just like to jump straight into things (this also includes me sometimes). There are a number of problems that arise from not planning properly before starting.

    One of those problems is the fact that it is difficult to document when you wish to reach particular milestones in your project if you haven’t put any thought into the route you would like to take to get to your end goal.

    Without proper planning even the end goal itself may not be that clear. Having a clear idea of what our end goal is, what it means, who it is for, and who it is not for, are all parts of the planning phase. Every web professional should have a set of questions that as a minimum should be answered before undertaking any web project. That way, there is a sense of clarity and direction from the very moment you start a web project.

    If you do not have any such questions I suggest you take a look at this article I read from Design Shack. I think this is a great starting point for anyone.

  3. Pick The Right Tools

    Okay, so the tool doesn’t make the web developer. However, tools are there to make the web developers job easier.

    There is more pressure now than ever before on web professionals to produce results in a fraction of the time they used to without compromising on the quality of the work. Picking the right tools is an important step to that end.

    I recently started using Visual Studio Code. It’s a very fully featured lightweight IDE from Microsoft. I know, lightweight and fully featured don’t normally go together.

    I like VSCode so much because it comes with the capability to download extensions to make working with whatever program you are working with much easier. It also comes with a built in command line interface, which works great for me as I like to use git and install some of my extensions using command line.

    While I would highly recommend using VSCode to anyone, I encourage you all to try different tools out for yourselves. If the tool is not right for you then discard it and exchange it for one that fits into your workflow better.

  4. Close Unnecessary Tabs

    This one is a problem for great many people. It’s so easy to end up with way too many browser tabs open. There is that video you need to watch on youtube, those articles you need to read, your research sources, online catalogues, social media… it goes on and on.

    Stop!

    This is precisely why bookmarking exists. Chances are, you are not going to actually get back to any of those browser tabs any time soon. It can all get very overwhelming and stressful.

    The more clutter we have around us the busier we feel. This applies in life and on your browser. There is simply not enough time in the day to get through everything all at once.

    Take the time to organise what you really need and bookmark it for later. If it’s really that important you can even set reminders for yourself to go back to it. But, if it stays in-front of you open then it is just another thing that you need to do that you don’t really have the time for. Either that or it will end up taking up time you should be using for something else.

    Either way, having too many browser tabs open is just a fast track way to stressing about how very busy you are.

  5. Don't Re-Invent the Wheel

    Often in development we find ourselves having to repeat the same piece of code again and again.

    Using code snippets saves time for the obvious reason that typing 6 words is faster than typing 20. However, it also saves you time because retyping entire bits of code leave you at risk of making human errors. A simple typo gone unnoticed could be the disastrous to your project and very time consuming.

    Using an editor that makes it easy to create and use your own snippets is the an amazing way to shave production time and free up time for other parts of your project.

  6. Ask For Help

    If you get stuck on a problem, yes you could find the answer in a book somewhere, or you could spend hours and hours trying to wreck your brain over what could have possibly gone wrong. I find the best way to deal with it is to ask for help.

    There is a vast and amazing community of talented web professionals online. If you don’t have the solution to your problem I assure you, someone else probably does, or if not, someone has the knowledge to point you in the right direction.

    As well as asking for help, it is great to also give back to the community. If you have a moment, take time to answer a question another web professional such as yourself might have. Helping other people is also another good way to keep that knowledge fresh in your brain.

  7. Never Stop Learning

    So someone told me that, “If you don’t enjoy learning new things, you have no business going into web development”. This statement couldn’t be more true.

    Web technologies are changing all the time and it is important for you to keep up. This doesn’t mean that you now need to be an expert on every web technology that hits the market; but you need to at the very least learn enough to keep yourself competitive in your field and niche.

    Luckily for us there are so many routes that we can take towards learning web technologies. Some are paid and some completely free. It’s just a matter of knowing what it is you want to learn and where you can most likely find it.

    Websites like Codecademy, Khan Academy, and Free Code Camp are great resources for gaining extra knowledge in web development and what’s best about those is that they are FREE!

    Ofcourse, there are other free and paid choices for you to consider. Just search for what you want to learn online and stay informed.

  8. Work In Sprints

    One of the many problems that web professionals face is that they often forget they are human. By that I mean I have met many that would attempt to work for hours and hours with no rest whatsoever. I myself have tried that.

    However, this is not at all good for our productivity. We might fool ourselves into believing that this is the best way to get our work done but that simply is not the case.

    I recommend working in for shorter periods of time were you only focus on one task avoiding any distractions. Set goals for each sprint and at the end, take a break. RELAX. Take your hands away from the keyboard. Better yet, just walk away from your computer (Or whatever it is that you were doing).

    Before you start the next sprint make sure you are clear on what the goals for that sprint are and prepare the any tools you may need. Your next sprint should be just as productive as the previous one.

  9. Rest

    So you have already been advised to take breaks but the importance of rest is something that so many of us take for granted. Some even believe that proper makes rest makes you lazy. That is why it gets its own special mention in this article.

    Rest is not only important for our overall health, it is also arguably essential for optimum productivity. Proper rest allows us to think more clearly and thus allowing us to do our work more effectively.

    45 minutes of work while well rested, refreshed, thinking clearly and focusing well is better than 2 hours of work on a tired, sluggish brain that lacks the ability to even focus on the work at hand let alone solve complex problems that arise during your project.

  10. Create Guides

    I mention this last but it is certainly not least in this list. When writing large amounts of code one of the most difficult things is to remain consistent throughout your code. One way to maintain consistency is to use guides.

    A web designer or developer can add a set of predesigned elements to the project and a set of rules and guidelines to be followed so that all the different parts of the website remain consistent.

    This is great for large projects that you may be working on or when multiple professionals are working on the same project.
